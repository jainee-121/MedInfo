PARACETAMOL

Introduction

Paracetamol, also known as acetaminophen in the United States and Canada, is a widely used medication for the relief of pain and fever.
Chemical Structure
Paracetamol is chemically known as N-acetyl-para-aminophenol. Its chemical formula is C8H9NO2.
Mechanism of Action
The exact mechanism of action of paracetamol is not fully understood, but it is believed to involve inhibition of prostaglandin synthesis in the central nervous system. Prostaglandins are chemicals in the body that promote inflammation and fever. By reducing their production, paracetamol helps to alleviate pain and lower fever.
Pharmacokinetics
Paracetamol is rapidly absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract after oral administration and reaches peak blood concentrations within 30 to 60 minutes. It is metabolized primarily in the liver and excreted in the urine, with a half-life of about 2 to 3 hours in adults.
Indications
Paracetamol is commonly used for the relief of mild to moderate pain, including headaches, toothaches, muscle aches, menstrual cramps, and backaches. It is also used to reduce fever associated with various conditions, such as colds, flu, and other infections.
Safety Profile
When used at recommended doses, paracetamol is generally considered safe and well-tolerated. However, overdose can lead to liver damage and even liver failure. It is essential to adhere to recommended dosages and avoid combining paracetamol with other medications containing acetaminophen to prevent accidental overdose.
Special Populations
Paracetamol is considered safe for use in children, pregnant women, and older adults when used as directed. However, dosage adjustments may be necessary in patients with liver or kidney impairment.
